Automobile Industry

The automobile industry is divided into four major business segments: two-wheelers, three-wheelers, commercial vehicles, and passenger vehicles. India is the 5th largest automobile market globally with annual production of 23M vehicles in FY22, contributing 7.1% to GDP.

Industry Size: $222B
CAGR (2022-27): 9%
Units Produced: 23M/year
Units Exported: 6M/year
GDP Contribution: 7.1%

Business Segments

  • Two-Wheelers: Mopeds, Scooters, Motorcycles (77% of volume)
  • Three-Wheelers: Passenger & Goods
  • Commercial Vehicles: Light CV, Heavy CV
  • Passenger Vehicles: Cars, Utility, Multi-purpose (18%)

Key Revenue & Cost Drivers

  • Revenue: Sales Volume, Product Mix, After Sales Service, Financing
  • Cost: Commodities (Raw Material), R&D, Distribution, Compliance
  • Macro: Fuel Prices, Policy Support, Market Sentiment, Inflation

Trends & Opportunities

  • Electric Vehicles (EV) adoption accelerating under FAME scheme
  • Autonomous Driving technology development
  • In-Car Technologies (infotainment, connectivity)
  • Shared Mobility (ride-hailing, subscription models)
  • Industry 4.0 & Smart Manufacturing

Challenges & Policy Updates

  • EV adoption barriers (charging infra, range anxiety)
  • AV regulatory framework still evolving
  • Supply chain disruptions (semiconductors)
  • FAME, CAFÉ-2, BS-VI schemes promoting EVs
  • Automotive Mission Plan 2016–2026 (AMP 2026) targets
  • PV sales recovered to 3.1M in FY22 after COVID lows

Value Chain Analysis

  • Pre-Production: R&D (designing, innovation), Raw Materials (commodities, technology), Inbound Logistics (mode, route)
  • During Production: Manufacturing Equipment, Assembly, Quality Control, Utilities & Labour
  • Post-Production: Outbound Logistics, Dealer Management, Salesforce, Advertising, Insurance, Repair & Rentals

Case Interview Frameworks

Master the essential frameworks used in consulting case interviews. Each framework is battle-tested in consulting interview rooms across top firms.

Profitability Framework

A profitability case examines the underlying reasons for a company's decline (or improvement) in profits. The fundamental equation: Profit = Revenue − Costs. Always confirm: Is the issue revenue-side, cost-side, or both? Is it industry-wide or firm-specific?

Core Issue Tree

PROFIT = REVENUE − COST
📈 Revenue Issues
Price × Volume Product Mix Market Share Market Size
📉 Cost Issues
Fixed Costs Variable Costs Operational Non-Operational
1
Clarify & Scope

Confirm problem statement. Ask: For how long? Which geography? Which product lines? Compare to industry or just internal trend?

2
Revenue Deep Dive

Revenue = Price × Quantity. Drill into: Has price changed? Has volume changed? Is it product mix? Segment by product/geography/customer to find the anomaly. Use customer journey (Awareness → Purchase → Retention) when conventional splits don't work.

3
Cost Deep Dive

Separate Fixed vs Variable costs. Within each, use Value Chain Analysis: R&D → Raw Materials → Production → Distribution → Marketing → After-Sales. Use PESTEL for external macro cost drivers.

4
Root Cause Identification

Is it internal (product quality, operations, pricing strategy) or external (competition, regulation, macroeconomics, customer preferences)? Validate with data provided.

5
Recommendations

Short-term (quick wins) vs Long-term (structural). Prioritize by Impact × Ease. Address both revenue enhancement and cost optimization. Conclude with risks.

Revenue Breakdown

  • Price (list price, discounts, mix)
  • # Customers × Purchase frequency × Order value
  • New vs Repeat customers
  • Market share × Market size
  • Segment-level analysis (B2B vs B2C, geography)

Cost Breakdown

  • Fixed: Rent, Salaries, Depreciation, Amortization
  • Variable: Raw Materials, Labour (direct), Commissions
  • Semi-variable: Utilities, Maintenance
  • Non-operational: Finance costs, Bad debts
  • Value chain mapping for cost localization
💡 💡 Pro Tips for Case Interviews: Always ask if the issue is industry-wide or company-specific first — this narrows your tree immediately. Use MECE buckets. When revenue is the issue, always split by segment before diving into price vs volume. Never jump to recommendations before finding the root cause.

Market Entry Framework

Market entry cases ask whether a company should enter a new geography, segment, or product category. The framework has four key stages: Qualifying Questions → Decision to Enter → Mode of Entry → Operationalizing.

A
Qualifying Questions (Scoping)

Understand the company: current geography, USP, target segment. Understand the motive: profit increase, market share, diversification, regulatory. Ask about constraints: time, capital, breakeven expectations.

B
Decision to Enter — Market Attractiveness

Quantitative: Market size & growth rate, Breakeven analysis (NPV, IRR, Payback period), Expected revenues & costs. Qualitative: Country/macro analysis (PESTEL), Industry analysis (Porter's 5 Forces), Need-gap analysis & STP (Segment → Target → Position).

C
Mode of Entry

Evaluate: Organic Growth (Greenfield), M&A (Acquisition), Joint Venture (JV), Licensing/Franchise. Rate each on: Costs, Time to profitability, Autonomy/Control, Risk. Consider on-shore vs off-shore, niche player acquisition, partnership with local bank/firm.

D
Operationalizing (Setup)

Establish the value chain: Sourcing → Manufacturing → Logistics → Distribution → Marketing → Customer Service. Apply 4P framework: Product (adapt vs standard?), Price (local pricing), Place (channels), Promotion (messaging).

Why this country/market specifically?
Why now — what triggered this decision?
What is our competitive advantage in this new market?
Are there regulatory/FDI restrictions?
What is the expected market share & timeline?
Do we have existing operations that overlap?
What is the risk appetite of the company?
Is there an existing player we can acquire?
💡 Pro Tip: Always ask about motive before the analysis. A defensive entry (blocking competition) needs a very different recommendation than an offensive entry (profit maximization). Industry best practice emphasizes: "Quantify the entry decision — always estimate Year 1 revenue and breakeven period."

Pricing Framework

Pricing cases involve setting the optimal price for a new or existing product/service. The three main pricing methodologies are Cost-Based, Competition-Based, and Value-Based. Cost is the floor; Value is the ceiling; Competition gives the range.

💰 Cost-Based Pricing (Floor)

  • R&D costs (amortized over product life)
  • Production costs: Fixed (factory, salaries) + Variable (materials, labour)
  • Breakeven period & WACC
  • Target margin / ROI requirements
  • Price = Cost per unit + Target margin

🏆 Value-Based Pricing (Ceiling)

  • Willingness-to-pay (WTP) of target customer
  • Opportunity cost for the customer
  • Benchmark against similar products/substitutes in market
  • Economic value created for customer (EVE model)
  • Price = Customer's WTP

⚔️ Competition-Based Pricing (Range)

  • Substitute products (pricing pressure from alternatives)
  • Direct competitors (existing products in category)
  • Price premium or discount vs competition (based on differentiation)
  • If highly differentiated → can command premium; commodity → price-taker

🌊 Demand/Supply-Based Pricing

  • Price elasticity of demand (how sensitive is quantity to price change?)
  • Dynamic pricing (surge pricing for perishables, events)
  • Yield management (airlines, hotels)
  • Finding the revenue-maximizing price point on demand curve
1
Understand the Product & Objective

What is the product? New or existing? Is the objective market penetration, margin maximization, or competitive defense?

2
Calculate Cost-Based Floor

Get fixed costs, variable costs, R&D amortization. Decide over how many units/years to recover fixed costs. Compute minimum viable price.

3
Estimate Value-Based Ceiling

Use customer segmentation to find WTP. Benchmark against substitutes. Consider economic value created.

4
Check Competition Range

Where are competitors priced? What is our differentiation that justifies deviation from competition pricing?

5
Set Final Price & Validate

Price must be between Cost Floor and WTP Ceiling. Validate: Does this price make sense for a rational consumer? Test with elasticity if needed.

What are the total fixed and variable costs?
Over how many units should fixed cost be recovered?
What is the customer's willingness to pay?
How are competitors pricing their products?
Is the product differentiated enough to command premium?
What is the price elasticity of demand?
💡 Pricing Strategies to Know: Penetration Pricing (low entry price to gain share), Skimming (high initial price, then drop), Freemium, Bundle pricing, Price discrimination (different prices for different segments). Always sense-check: "Would a rational customer pay this?"

Growth Strategy Framework

Growth strategy cases involve helping a company achieve a target growth rate (e.g., "double revenue in 5 years"). Use the Ansoff Matrix as the primary structure: Existing vs New (Products × Geographies). Then evaluate Organic vs Inorganic growth levers.

Ansoff Matrix — Growth Options

✅ Market Penetration (Existing Product × Existing Market)

Increase market share in current market. Levers: 4Ps (Price promotions, Product upgrades, Distribution expansion, Advertising).

🆕 Product Development (New Product × Existing Market)

Launch new products to existing customers. R&D investment, innovation pipeline, licensing technology.

🌍 Market Development (Existing Product × New Market)

Expand to new geographies or customer segments with existing product. (Overlap with Market Entry Framework.)

🚀 Diversification (New Product × New Market)

Highest risk. Core vs Non-Core offerings. Related vs Unrelated diversification. Usually via M&A.

📗 Organic Growth Levers

  • Improve own competencies & efficiencies
  • Pricing & promotion strategies (incentives, awareness)
  • Distribution channel expansion (new channels, contracts)
  • Core offering enhancements (features, after-sales)
  • Non-core services & accessories
  • Digital transformation to reduce cost & improve reach

📘 Inorganic Growth Levers

  • M&A (Mergers & Acquisitions) — fastest but costliest
  • Joint Ventures (JV) — shared risk, shared control
  • Partnerships (distribution, technology, brand)
  • Franchise model (asset-light expansion)
  • Licensing (IP monetization)
1
Align on Growth Target

What is the target growth rate? By what timeline? Revenue growth, market share, or profitability? Is growth organic, inorganic, or both?

2
Diagnose Current State

What is the current growth rate? Why is it insufficient? Internal constraints (capacity, product) or external (market saturation, competition)?

3
Generate Growth Options (Ansoff Matrix)

Systematically evaluate all four quadrants. List specific initiatives under each.

4
Prioritize & Quantify

Rate options on: Revenue potential, Feasibility, Timeline, Risk. Quantify top 2-3 initiatives. Build to the target growth number.

5
Recommend & Implementation Roadmap

Short-term quick wins + Long-term strategic moves. Flag risks and dependencies.

💡 Key Insight: Always confirm whether the growth target is absolute (revenue $X) or relative (X% CAGR). Build a simple model: Current Revenue × Growth Rate = Gap to close. Then show how your recommendations fill the gap. Industry experts note: "Always link recommendations back to the numbers."

M&A / PE Due Diligence Framework

M&A cases involve evaluating whether to acquire a target company or invest (PE/VC). Use the SMIOLF framework for evaluation, along with financial valuation and cultural/strategic fit assessment.

1
Scoping Questions

Investment objective: Exit strategy / Growth / Strategic Diversification? Fund style: Debt or Equity? Controlling or Non-Controlling stake? Existing portfolio synergies?

2
SMIOLF Framework

Strategic Fit (vision alignment, market position), Market Analysis (industry attractiveness, growth), Investment Financials (valuation, multiples, IRR), Operational Due Diligence (value chain, efficiencies), Legal & Regulatory (compliance, IP, liabilities), Fit (cultural alignment, leadership).

3
Synergy Analysis

Revenue synergies (cross-selling, new markets). Cost synergies (economies of scale, shared infrastructure, headcount rationalization). Check: Combined A+B > standalone A and B.

4
Valuation Methods

DCF (Discounted Cash Flow), Comparable Company Analysis (trading multiples), Precedent Transactions (deal multiples). Key ratios: EV/EBITDA, P/E, P/BV depending on industry.

5
Deal Structure & Risks

All-cash vs stock deal? Earn-out clauses? Post-merger integration plan. Risk factors: PMI complexity, cultural clash, regulatory approvals (CCI in India).

💡 Key Metrics for M&A: EBITDA multiples vary by industry (Tech: 15-25x, FMCG: 8-12x, Manufacturing: 5-8x). Always ask: "What is the strategic rationale beyond financials?" Often the interviewer wants you to challenge a deal that looks good on paper but has cultural or integration risks.

General Frameworks & Tools

Beyond the core case-type frameworks, these analytical tools are used across all case types. Mastering these will significantly elevate your case performance.

PESTEL Analysis (Macro)

  • Political: Government stability, trade policies, FDI norms
  • Economic: GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, FX
  • Social: Demographics, consumer trends, cultural factors
  • Technological: Innovation, digital disruption, R&D
  • Environmental: ESG, climate regulations, carbon targets
  • Legal: Regulations, IP laws, labour laws, compliance

Porter's Five Forces (Micro)

  • Threat of New Entrants: Barriers to entry (capital, regulation, IP)
  • Bargaining Power of Buyers: Price sensitivity, switching costs
  • Bargaining Power of Suppliers: Concentration, substitutes for inputs
  • Threat of Substitutes: Alternative products/services
  • Industry Rivalry: Number of players, price wars, differentiation

4P Framework (Marketing)

  • Product: Features, quality, packaging, brand, USP
  • Price: Strategy (penetration/skimming), discounts, loyalty
  • Place: Distribution channels, supply chain, retail placement
  • Promotion: ATL/BTL advertising, PR, personal selling, digital

Value Chain Analysis

  • R&D → Raw Material Procurement → Inbound Logistics
  • Production (Manufacturing, QC, Assembly)
  • Outbound Logistics → Distribution → Marketing → Sales
  • After-Sales Service
  • For each stage: Man, Machine, Method, Measurement

BCG Matrix (Portfolio)

  • Stars: High market share, high growth → Invest
  • Cash Cows: High share, low growth → Harvest
  • Question Marks: Low share, high growth → Decide
  • Dogs: Low share, low growth → Divest

Breakeven & NPV Analysis

  • Breakeven = Fixed Investment ÷ Annual Profit Margin
  • Annual Revenue = Demand × Price
  • Annual Profit = Revenue − Fixed Costs − (Units × VC)
  • NPV = Σ [Cash Flow / (1+r)ᵗ] − Initial Investment
  • IRR: Discount rate where NPV = 0

🧭 🧭 Case Hygiene — Golden Rules for Consulting Interviews

  • Always clarify the problem statement before jumping in — never assume
  • Be MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) in every structure you build
  • Talk through your math — never calculate silently; narrate each step
  • Sanity check your numbers: Does 1 crore customers per day make sense?
  • Mistakes are okay — recover gracefully, don't repeat them
  • Drive the case — don't wait for the interviewer to tell you where to go next
  • Conclude with: Summary → Recommendation → Implementation → Risks
💡 Internal vs External Buckets: Whenever you're stuck on root cause, default to Internal (product, process, brand, leadership, pricing) vs External (competition, government regulation, macroeconomics, customer behavior change). This MECE bucket works for almost any case type.

A: Cost-based: ₹50 / (1 - 50%) = ₹100. This is the floor price.

PricingChallengingInfrastructure / Transport
Case 24: Delhi-Amritsar Expressway — Toll Tax Pricing
▼
Problem Statement: A construction company built a Delhi-Amritsar expressway under BOT model (Build-Operate-Transfer). They must determine the toll tax to recover $2.5B investment + 20% profit over 10 years.
Key Facts: 8-10 entry/exit points. All vehicle types (trucks, cars, public transport). 2 competing highways. 4 similar expressways in India. Saves 2 hours of travel time.
Q: Three pricing methods — which to apply?
A: All three — triangulate for final recommendation.
Q: Cost-based — structure?
A: Fixed costs (construction $2.5B + land) + Variable costs (maintenance, labour, toll booth over 10 years). Divide by total vehicle-miles driven over 10 years. Add load factor for heavy vehicles. Price = (Total Cost + 20% profit) ÷ Vehicle Miles.
Q: Competition-based?
A: Price between competitor highway toll (lower bound) and similar expressway toll (upper bound, since we save 2 hours). Our expressway commands premium over highway.
Q: Value-based?
A: Value of 2 hours saved per user. For truck: time value of cargo + driver. For passenger car: leisure/business value. Add this to base/competitor price as maximum WTP premium.
💡 Takeaway: Infrastructure pricing always uses all 3 methods. Cost-based gives the floor (minimum to recover investment). Value-based (time saved) gives the ceiling. Competition (similar expressways, highways) gives the range. Final price sits in the overlap.

Tips & Tricks for Consulting Interviews

Curated from top consulting firms' interview processes — everything from cracking the case to acing the fit round and negotiating your offer.

📋 Case Interview Do's & Don'ts

The difference between candidates who get offers and those who don't often comes down to these fundamentals.

✅ DO These

  • Always clarify and repeat the problem statement before diving in
  • Ask for 1-2 minutes to structure your thoughts — always take it
  • Think out loud — narrate every step of your reasoning
  • Use MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) in every bucket
  • Listen actively and adapt when the interviewer gives hints
  • Develop your own framework — don't force-fit a memorized one
  • Focus on high-impact issues (80-20 rule)
  • Make quick, accurate mental calculations — round numbers to simplify
  • Drive the case forward — take ownership of the direction
  • Give a structured recommendation at the end: Problem → Finding → Action → Risk
  • Show genuine enthusiasm for solving the business problem

❌ DON'T Do These

  • Jump straight to conclusions without structure
  • Memorize and recite frameworks robotically
  • Calculate silently — always narrate math steps
  • Panic when you don't know an answer — say "let me think about this"
  • Vehemently defend a wrong analysis — admit and move forward
  • Ask too many questions before structuring (max 3-4 clarifying questions)
  • Repeat the same mistake twice
  • Internalize your thought process — interviewers can't grade silence
  • Make logically correct but practically infeasible recommendations
  • Ignore the numbers — always validate your logic with data
  • Be rude, dismissive, or overconfident with the interviewer

🎯 The 7-Step Case Interview Approach

1
Repeat & Clarify Objective

Restate the problem in your own words. Confirm the metric of success (profits? market share? cost reduction?). Get all relevant initial information in 2-3 questions maximum.

2
Set Context with Smart Questions

Ask targeted questions that reveal the case structure: "Is this industry-wide or company-specific?", "Which geography?", "Since when?", "How does the client make money?"

3
Take Time to Lay Down Structure

Say: "I'd like to take a minute to structure my approach." Build a MECE issue tree on your notepad. Present the structure before diving in — get buy-in from the interviewer.

4
Involve Interviewer in Analysis

Don't go silent and analyze alone. Narrate: "I'd like to look at revenue first because it seems more likely to be the root cause. Is that okay?" This shows judgment and consulting communication skills.

5
Deep Dive & Diagnose

Use your issue tree to drill down. When you hit the root cause, validate it: "So the issue is X, which is caused by Y, which we see in the data because Z." Make the chain of logic explicit.

6
Make Specific, Prioritized Recommendations

Don't give generic advice. Structure as: Short-term (0-6 months) + Long-term (6-24 months). Prioritize by impact × feasibility. Always have a rationale for each recommendation.

7
Summarize Concisely

End with: "To summarize: the client's issue is X, caused by Y. I recommend A in the short-term and B in the long-term. The key risk to watch is C." Brief, structured, confident.

💬 Fit & HR Interview — STAR Framework

Almost every consulting firm spends 20-40% of interview time on "fit" or behavioral questions. These assess whether you have the right personal qualities to be a consultant. Use the STAR framework for structured answers.

🌍
SITUATION
Set the context — when, where, what was the challenge?
🎯
TASK
What was YOUR specific responsibility? (Not "we did")
⚡
ACTION
What specific steps did YOU take? Focus on analytical + leadership actions.
📊
RESULT
Quantify the impact: "Revenue increased by X%" or "Saved ₹Y".

Common Fit Questions

  • "Tell me about yourself" — Start with present, go to past, end with future ambition
  • "Why consulting?" — Impact, variety of problems, learning curve
  • "Why [firm name specifically]?" — Research firm values, recent projects, culture
  • "Greatest achievement?" — Quantify impact; show leadership and analytical skills
  • "Tell me about a failure" — Own it, show learning, show you course-corrected
  • "Why now?" — MBA/PG timing, experience inflection point
  • "Tell me about a conflict" — Show maturity, empathy, resolution skills

Firm-Specific Tips

  • McKinsey PEI: 3 dimensions — Personal Impact, Entrepreneurial Drive, Inclusive Leadership. Prepare 3 deep STAR stories for each.
  • BCG: Heavy on analytical reasoning in fit. "How do you solve problems?" is a real question.
  • Bain: Culture-fit is paramount. "BAIN values" — genuine interest in people and results.
  • Big 4: Industry knowledge matters more. Know their recent flagship projects and sector focus areas.
  • Boutiques: Show genuine intellectual curiosity in the niche area (strategy, operations, digital).

🗣️ Communication & Presence in Interviews

💡
Structure before you speak

Never start talking before you know where you're going. Use filler phrases to buy time: "That's an interesting question — let me think about this for a moment." Then structure in your head before opening your mouth.

💡
Pyramid Principle (Answer First)

Start with the conclusion, then give the reasoning. "I believe the company should enter the market — here are three reasons: 1. Market is attractive, 2. We have competitive advantage, 3. Timing is favorable." Don't build up to the answer.

💡
Quantify everything

Vague: "The market is large." Better: "The market is approximately ₹50,000 Crore and growing at 15% annually." Consultants quantify — it's a signal of rigor. Even if unsure, give a range with reasoning.

💡
Pause is power

Silence is not weakness — it signals you're thinking, not panicking. 5-10 seconds of thoughtful silence before answering a hard question is far better than rushing to a wrong answer.

💡
Recover from mistakes gracefully

When you make an error: "Actually, let me revisit that calculation — I made an error in [X]. The correct answer should be [Y]." Recovery without self-flagellation shows composure — a key consulting trait.

🧮 Mental Math Shortcuts for Interviews

Rounding Strategies

  • Round to clean numbers: 1,47,000 → 1.5 lakh; 2.4B → 2.5B
  • Use 10% increments for percentages (easier mental math)
  • When multiplying large numbers: separate and add (e.g., 37 × 24 = 40×24 − 3×24 = 960−72 = 888)
  • Zeros: always manage zeroes explicitly. Say "ten to the power 6" not "million" to avoid confusion
  • Cross-check: estimate answer before calculating to catch order-of-magnitude errors

Useful Conversion Facts

  • 1 Crore = 10 Million = 10,000,000
  • 1 Lakh = 100 Thousand = 100,000
  • 1 Billion = 100 Crore = 1,000 Million
  • 1 USD ≈ ₹83 (use ₹80 for easy math)
  • 1 sq km = 1,000,000 sq meters = 100 hectares
  • 1 working year ≈ 250 days, 10 hrs/day = 2,500 hrs/year
  • Average Indian salary (mid-income): ₹5-8 lakh/year ≈ ₹500/hr

🏆 What Separates Great Candidates from Good Ones

1
Business Judgment over Mechanical Frameworks

Average candidates run through frameworks mechanically. Great candidates use frameworks as tools and show genuine business instinct: "I'd prioritize the revenue side first because this is an industry where demand is cyclical and costs are largely fixed." The "why" behind your structure impresses interviewers.

2
Client-Friendly Communication

Consulting is about advising clients, not proving you're smart. Great candidates communicate like they're talking to a business leader, not a professor. Use simple, direct language. Avoid jargon. Pause to check understanding. "Does that make sense?" shows collaborative instinct.

3
Genuine Curiosity About the Business Problem

Interviewers can tell the difference between someone who prepared 50 cases and someone who actually finds the business problem interesting. Read business news daily. When the case involves an industry you know, bring real-world insights naturally — "Interestingly, I read that X industry is facing Y challenge..."

4
Composure Under Ambiguity

Consulting cases are deliberately ambiguous. Great candidates embrace ambiguity: "I don't have that data, but let me make a reasonable assumption and flag it." They don't freeze when information is missing. They proceed with stated assumptions and invite correction.

5
Synthesis Over Summary

When concluding a case, don't just summarize what you did. Synthesize: connect findings to implications. "We found that the root cause is X. This means the client needs to prioritize Y because if they don't, the risk of Z is significant." The "so what" is what gets you the offer.

💎 Final Reminder: Consulting interview prep is a marathon, not a sprint. The candidates who get offers are not the smartest in the room — they are the most prepared, the most structured, and the most composed under pressure. Practice daily. Seek feedback relentlessly. And remember: the interviewer is rooting for you to succeed.
