🎯 1. Asking Preliminary Questions — The Most Underestimated Skill ▼

Preliminary questions are not just about clarifying the problem statement — they actively help you understand the client's company and scope out the problem. This is the most underestimated part of the case interview. Its importance cannot be stressed enough.


The reason we use the phrase "Preliminary Questions" instead of "Clarifying Questions" is because you should not only be clarifying the initial problem statement but actively seeking pieces of information which help in understanding the client's company. It is not only about re-confirming information the interviewer has given, but also seeking information they have NOT given.


Types of Preliminary Questions

  • Clarify Objectives: In profitability cases — "Why is this a problem? What does the client want to achieve?" In market entry cases — "What metric defines success for entering this market?"
  • Understand the Product: "What exactly is the product/service?" Don't assume. A "watch manufacturer" could mean wristwatches, wall clocks, or alarm clocks — each has very different dynamics.
  • Understand the Value Chain: "Where does our client sit in the value chain — are they a manufacturer, distributor, or retailer?" This tells you what the company can and cannot control.
  • Geography: "Which markets are affected — specific cities, regions, or all of India?"
  • Industry vs. Company: "Is this problem specific to our client, or is it an industry-wide issue?" This single question can save enormous time in directing analysis.
  • Spidey-Sense Info: Any technical term you are not 100% clear on — ask how it is specifically defined and calculated. For example, "delinquency rate" — understand exactly how it is measured.
Pro Tip: Most preliminary questions have simple Yes/No answers. Ask 4–6 crisp questions in rapid succession. They cost very little time but can dramatically redirect and accelerate your case.
🗺️ 2. Having and Conveying an Overall Strategy ▼

After asking preliminary questions, lay out your Overall Strategy before diving into analysis. This is like a Table of Contents for the case interview — it tells the interviewer exactly what you plan to do in the next 25–30 minutes.


Why It Matters

The biggest advantage is seen in cases with no set pattern — market entry, unconventional cases. Without a stated strategy, you could spend time analyzing issues that don't tie back to the objective. If your approach is wrong, laying it out gives the interviewer a chance to correct you early, saving time.


Example (Market Entry Case)

"First, I will estimate the potential profits that can be made in this market. After establishing that this will be a sufficiently profitable venture, I will find out the feasibility of setting up the entire value chain. Finally, we can discuss the potential challenges, after which I will give my final recommendation."

Sub-Strategies and Mini-Syntheses

At each checkpoint between sections: (a) give a sub-strategy for the next part, and (b) give a mini-synthesis drawing inferences from the previous part. Example: "We have found that potential profits are $50M over 2 years, which meets our break-even threshold. Now I want to assess value chain feasibility — specifically, [1] production, [2] distribution, [3] marketing."

🔬 3. Problem Isolation Tools — Segmentation, Hypothesis & Industry vs. Company ▼

MECE Segmentation

Every segmentation must be Mutually Exclusive and Collectively Exhaustive. Never segment through trial and error. Always use logical, structured splits: by product line, by geography, by customer segment, by distribution channel, by value chain stage.

Value Chain Segmentation (Most Powerful Tool)

After identifying a volume problem, always segment along the value chain before jumping to the Business Situation Framework:

Production Issue (Can we produce as much?) → Distribution Push Issue (Are distributors/retailers pushing our product?) → Customer Pull Issue (Is there reduced consumer demand?)

This narrows the problem quickly. If you can isolate it to "Distribution Push," you don't need the full Business Situation Framework — just analyze distribution-specific issues.

Hypothesis-Driven Approach

Form a hypothesis and test it, rather than asking questions randomly. This demonstrates structured thinking. Example:

"Revenue has declined. This could be due to a price decline or a volume decline. [Ask] Do we know how price and volume have changed?"

"Since volume is down, it could be because the market size reduced or our market share declined. [Ask] Has the overall market size changed?"

Company-Specific vs. Industry-Wide Issue

Always check early whether the problem is company-specific or industry-wide. If the entire industry is facing a profit decline, you focus your analysis on industry factors. If only your client is affected, you focus on company-specific operations, costs, and products. This single question can eliminate half the potential causes immediately.

⚡ 4. Improving Drill Speed — 80/20, Benchmarking & Business Judgment ▼

The 80/20 Rule

Analyze the branch responsible for the largest portion of the problem first. Example: costs are up 50%, raw materials = 60% of cost, manufacturing = 40% of cost. If both increased 50%, analyze raw materials first. But if manufacturing cost doubled while raw materials rose only 15%, analyze manufacturing first — even though raw materials is the bigger cost bucket in absolute terms. Always prioritize by the size of the problem, not the size of the bucket.

Competitor Benchmarking

Benchmarking your client's metrics against competitors quickly identifies where they are lagging. Example: if distribution cost is 2× the industry average, but production and marketing costs are similar, focus your analysis on distribution — even if production cost is higher in absolute terms.

Business Judgment and Insights

Use business insight to prioritize which branches to analyze. Example: for a laptop manufacturer losing market share, the most likely differentiators are price, specifications, and brand image — not geography or sales rep count. Use this to quickly jump to the relevant analysis rather than going through an exhaustive checklist.

Pro Tip: Business judgment improves with reading — Economic Times, Business Standard, and business magazines are excellent sources. Make it a habit to follow business news daily during preparation.
📋 5. CV and Resume Tips for Consulting Roles ▼

Top consulting firms are known to shortlist candidates whose CVs show 3–4 'spikes' — areas where the candidate has demonstrated exceptional performance or unique achievement.


Key CV Principles

  • Impact: Quantify everything. "Increased sales by 23% over 6 months" is far stronger than "helped increase sales." Every point should show measurable impact where possible.
  • Language: Use crisp, clear language. Avoid jargon that non-specialists won't understand. If your role was highly technical, translate it into business impact.
  • Structure: Order boxes tactfully — Education, Academic Achievements, Work Experience, Extra-Curriculars, Positions of Responsibility. Lead with your strongest section.
  • Highlighting: Use bold to draw attention to the most impressive parts of each point. Selectors review hundreds of CVs — make key achievements immediately visible.
  • Formatting: Clean, consistent formatting is non-negotiable. Poor formatting creates a negative first impression of your attention to detail.
  • One Page: Keep to one page for most MBA/undergrad applications. Every word must earn its space.

Action Verbs to Lead CV Points

Led, Managed, Delivered, Achieved, Designed, Built, Grew, Reduced, Optimised, Implemented, Spearheaded, Launched, Streamlined, Generated, Negotiated, Analysed, Developed, Coordinated

🗣️ 6. Behavioural / HR Question Preparation ▼

Behavioral questions assess personality, values, and fit. Prepare structured, story-based answers using the STAR method (Situation → Task → Action → Result).


Most Frequently Asked Questions

  • Tell me about yourself (craft a 90-second narrative)
  • Why consulting? Why this specific role (Strategy / Operations / M&A)?
  • Tell me about a time you led a team through a difficult situation
  • Describe a time you had to persuade someone with a different point of view
  • Tell me about your greatest professional achievement
  • What is your biggest weakness? How are you addressing it?
  • Where do you see yourself in 5 years?
  • Tell me about a time you failed and what you learned
  • Why do you want to leave your current role?
  • What skills from your background make you a strong consulting candidate?

STAR Method Structure

S — Situation: Set the scene briefly (1–2 sentences)
T — Task: What was your specific role or challenge?
A — Action: What specific steps did YOU take? (Use "I" not "we")
R — Result: What was the measurable outcome? What did you learn?
🏁 7. Final Synthesis and Recommendation ▼

Always close the case with a structured recommendation. The typical format is:


"Based on my analysis, I recommend [X] because: first, [reason 1]; second, [reason 2]; third, [reason 3]. The top risk to this recommendation is [Y], and I would mitigate it by [Z]. If I had more time, I would also look at [additional area]."

Key Principles for Final Recommendation

  • Lead with the recommendation — don't build up to it. State it first, then support it.
  • Give exactly 3 supporting reasons (the "Rule of 3" is a consulting communication standard)
  • Acknowledge risks and how to address them — this shows mature judgment
  • Be decisive. Interviewers want to see you take a stance, not hedge indefinitely.
  • Link back to the client's stated goal — make sure your recommendation directly solves their problem
  • Keep it concise — 60–90 seconds maximum
🧮 8. Mental Math and Quantitative Tips ▼

Quantitative fluency in consulting interviews means being able to work through numbers quickly, clearly, and accurately under pressure.


Key Techniques

  • Round aggressively: Use round numbers to simplify calculations. Interviewers care about your approach and order of magnitude — not precision to 2 decimal places.
  • State your assumptions aloud: "I'll assume India's population is 1.4 billion, urban population is 35%..." This builds confidence and lets the interviewer correct you early if needed.
  • Build step by step: Never jump to a number. Walk through each intermediate step: "Urban households = 500M / 4 persons = 125M households."
  • Sanity check: Does your final answer make intuitive sense? "300 million umbrellas sold per year in India — that's roughly one every 5 years per person. Sounds reasonable."
  • Percentage shortcuts: 10% = divide by 10. 5% = half of 10%. 25% = divide by 4. 33% ≈ divide by 3. Practice these until they're instant.
  • Avoid complex fractions: Convert to decimals or percentages. Don't leave messy fractions in your working.

Common Calculation Errors to Avoid

  • Mixing units (crore vs million vs billion) — always standardise
  • Applying percentages to the wrong base
  • Forgetting to account for penetration rates
  • Not accounting for market segments separately (treating all consumers as homogeneous)
💬 9. Non-Verbal Communication and Interview Presence ▼
  • Thinking time: It is perfectly acceptable to say "Give me a moment to structure my thoughts" and take 30–60 seconds before answering. Structured thinking beats rushing into a wrong answer.
  • Paper and pen: Always bring paper. Write your framework structure clearly. Interviewers often ask to see your working sheet.
  • Tone and pacing: Speak clearly at a measured pace. Rushing signals nervousness. A calm, deliberate tone signals confidence.
  • Eye contact: Maintain natural eye contact. Avoid looking down too frequently while talking.
  • Active listening: When the interviewer gives information, nod and acknowledge. Repeat key data points to confirm: "So revenue declined by 20% — is that correct?"
  • Don't give up: If you get stuck, say "I may be approaching this from the wrong angle — let me re-examine my structure." This shows intellectual honesty and resilience, which interviewers value highly.
📅 10. Preparation Timeline and Study Plan ▼

Recommended 8-Week Prep Plan

Week Focus Actions
1–2 Foundation Learn frameworks (Profitability, Market Entry, Pricing, Growth, M&A). Read first 3–4 chapters of this guide. Do 5 easy cases.
3–4 Framework Application Do 2 cases per day (mix of types). Focus on asking strong preliminary questions and stating clear overall strategies. Start guesstimate practice (2 per day).
5–6 Speed and Refinement Practice under time pressure (30 mins per case). Start studying industry sectors. Do hard and challenging cases. Book 1 mock interview session.
7 Mock Interviews 2–3 mock interviews with feedback. Focus on behavioral questions. Refine CV. Review all frameworks for gaps.
8 Final Prep Light practice (1 case per day), review notes, revise industry sector KPIs. Rest well before interview day. Confidence building.

Most Important: Case prep is a skill, not knowledge — it improves only through practice. Aim for 40–50 cases minimum before your interview, with at least 10–15 under live mock conditions with real feedback.
🎓 11. Role-Specific Preparation — Strategy, M&A, Operations Consulting ▼

Strategy Consulting

Cases typically involve market entry, growth strategy, and competitive positioning. Focus on frameworks like Ansoff Matrix, Porter's Five Forces, and profitability analysis. Emphasis on quantitative rigor and sharp strategic insights. Expect complex, multi-part cases.

M&A (Deals / Transaction Advisory)

Cases focus on acquisition rationale, synergy estimation, valuation, and integration risk. Brush up on basic financial concepts: EBITDA, DCF, revenue multiples, payback period. Due diligence cases are common — look for red flags in target companies. Commercial due diligence involves assessing market size, competitive positioning, and growth sustainability.

Operations Consulting

Focus on cost reduction, process efficiency, supply chain, and operational improvement. Value chain analysis and lean/six sigma concepts are useful. Cases may involve factory throughput, logistics optimisation, workforce productivity, or digital transformation. Emphasise practical, implementable solutions.

Key Tip: Before any interview, understand the specific type of work done by that team — strategy, operations, digital, or deals. Tailor your "Why this role?" answer and your case preparation accordingly.
⚠️ 12. Common Mistakes to Avoid ▼
  • Force-fitting frameworks: Don't say "I'll use the Market Entry Framework." Say "I want to understand first whether this market is financially attractive, then whether we can realistically execute." Frameworks are internal tools, not scripts to recite.
  • Jumping to analysis without preliminary questions: Spending 20 minutes on the wrong part of the problem because you didn't ask "Is this company-specific or industry-wide?" at the start.
  • Not segmenting before using Business Situation Framework: Always isolate the problem first (by value chain, geography, product line) before asking broad environment questions.
  • Asking too many non-goal-oriented questions: Every question should move you closer to the case objective. If it doesn't, don't ask it.
  • Being too silent: Think out loud as much as possible. Interviewers want to see your reasoning process, not just your answer.
  • Giving up or backtracking dramatically: If you realise you've gone down the wrong path, acknowledge it calmly and redirect: "Let me reconsider my approach — I may have jumped ahead."
  • Weak or hedged recommendations: "It depends" is not a recommendation. Take a position, justify it, and acknowledge the main risk.
  • Ignoring the human element: Remember the interviewer is also assessing whether they'd want to work with you and whether you'd work well with clients. Be warm, collaborative, and genuinely curious.
