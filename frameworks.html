📈 1. Profitability Framework▼

When to use: Client experiencing profit decline, margin compression, or revenue stagnation.


Core Structure: Profit = Revenue − Costs

Revenue = Price × Volume

  • Price change? (Strategy shift, competition, discounts, mix effect)
  • Volume change? (Which product, geography, channel, customer segment?)

Costs = Fixed + Variable

  • Fixed: Rent, salaries, depreciation — don't change with output
  • Variable: Raw materials, packaging, logistics — change with output

The Evolved Approach — Value Chain Isolation

After identifying a volume problem, isolate across the value chain before applying Business Situation Framework:

Production Issue → Distribution Push Issue → Customer Pull Issue
  • Production: Capacity reduced? Quality issue? Raw material disruption? Labour shortage?
  • Distribution Push: Distributors/retailers not pushing our product? Lower margins vs competitors? Packaging issue? Shelf visibility? Stock-outs?
  • Customer Pull: Consumer preference shifted? New competitor product? Price sensitivity? Brand weakened? Seasonality?

Key Preliminary Questions

  • What exactly is the product and where does the client sit in the value chain?
  • Over what time period has the problem occurred?
  • Is this company-specific or an industry-wide issue? (This single question can eliminate half the analysis)
  • Which geography / product lines / customer segments are affected?
  • What is the client's goal — turnaround, specific margin target, cost benchmarking?

Common Segmentation Levers

By Product Line | By Geography | By Customer Segment (income, age, B2B vs B2C) | By Distribution Channel | By Time Period (seasonality)

🌐 2. Market Entry Framework▼

When to use: Client considering entering a new market — geography, product category, or customer segment.


Two-Part Evolved Structure

Part 1 — Economic Sense

Estimated Profit = (Market Size) × (Expected Market Share) × (Profit per Unit) − Fixed Costs

  • Market Size: Total market size, growth rate, segment breakdown
  • Market Share: Competitive landscape, barriers to entry, our differentiation
  • Unit Economics: Price, variable cost, contribution margin, investment required

Part 2 — Implementation Feasibility

Value Chain Check: Production → Distribution → Marketing

  • Capital, land, labour, equipment availability to meet demand
  • Distribution channels available vs required
  • Marketing investment for brand building in new market
  • Entry mode: Organic build vs Acquisition vs Joint Venture

Porter's Five Forces — Market Attractiveness

  • Supplier Power: Concentration, switching costs, substitute inputs
  • Buyer Power: Price sensitivity, volume, switching costs, substitutes available
  • Threat of Entrants: Capital requirements, regulations, brand loyalty, economies of scale
  • Threat of Substitutes: Performance vs price of alternatives
  • Competitive Rivalry: Number of players, growth rate, product differentiation, exit barriers

Critical Clarifying Questions

  • "What metric defines success for this market entry — profit target, market share, or strategic rationale?"
  • "Is there a specific reason the client is considering THIS market?"
  • "Is the client already present in an adjacent market or geography?"
  • "What is the timeline and risk appetite?"
💰 3. Pricing Strategy Framework▼

When to use: Client needs to price a new product, re-price an existing product, or evaluate pricing strategy.


1. Cost-Plus

Price = Total Cost/unit + Desired Margin%. Simple but ignores market dynamics. Best for commodities or regulated industries.

2. Competitor-Based

Price relative to competitors — premium, parity, or discount — based on differentiation. Risk: price wars erode margins.

3. Value-Based

Price = Willingness to Pay (WTP). Best for differentiated/new products. Capture surplus between cost and WTP.

Pricing Boundaries

Price Floor = Variable Cost per Unit (below this = loss on every unit). Price Ceiling = Customer WTP or best substitute price. Ideal price sits between floor and ceiling, determined by strategy and competitive position.

Additional Pricing Models to Consider

Dynamic pricing | Tiered/segmented pricing | Bundle pricing | Freemium (free base + paid premium) | Subscription | Price discrimination across channels

Revenue Growth Levers

Revenue = Price × Volume. Grow by: (1) Increasing price — premium positioning, value-added features; (2) Increasing volume via penetration — better distribution, promotions, customer acquisition; (3) Market development — new geographies, new segments, new channels; (4) Product development — new SKUs, adjacent products; (5) Inorganic — M&A, partnerships to acquire customers or capabilities.

🤝 5. Mergers & Acquisitions (M&A) Framework▼

When to use: Client considering acquiring a company, merging with a competitor, or being acquired. Also applicable for due diligence cases.


1. Strategic Rationale

  • Why this acquisition? Access to new market, technology, talent, or to eliminate a competitor?
  • Does it align with long-term strategic goals?

2. Synergies

  • Revenue: Cross-selling, new customer access, new geographies, combined brand value, product bundling
  • Cost: Economies of scale, elimination of duplicate functions, shared infrastructure, procurement leverage, headcount rationalisation

3. Valuation

  • Revenue multiples, EBITDA multiples, or DCF — what methodology is appropriate?
  • Is the asking price justified given the synergies? Payback period: when do synergies recover the acquisition premium?

4. Risks

  • Integration: culture clash, IT incompatibility, talent retention
  • Regulatory: antitrust approvals, sector-specific restrictions
  • Operational: customer attrition post-merger, supply chain disruptions
  • Financial: debt burden, reduced cash flow flexibility

5. Integration Plan

  • Day 1 vs. 100-day plan: immediate changes vs. phased integration
  • Organisational structure, reporting lines, leadership team
  • Brand strategy: combined brand, sub-brand, or separate brands?
⚙️ 6. Operations & Cost Reduction Framework▼

When to use: Client wants to reduce costs, improve operational efficiency, fix a supply chain bottleneck, or improve productivity.


Cost Value Chain Analysis

Map costs across the value chain: Inbound Logistics → Operations/Production → Outbound Logistics → Marketing & Sales → After-Sales Service. Identify where costs are disproportionate vs industry benchmarks.

Cost Reduction Levers

  • Labour: Automation, outsourcing, workforce optimisation, productivity improvement programmes
  • Raw Materials: Supplier renegotiation, alternate sourcing, bulk procurement, vertical integration
  • Energy: Efficiency upgrades, renewable energy transition, energy audits
  • Logistics: Route optimisation, load consolidation, 3PL partnerships, warehouse automation
  • Overheads: Real estate rationalisation, shared services, digitisation of admin processes
  • Manufacturing: Process re-engineering, waste reduction, capacity utilisation improvement, defect reduction

Key Diagnostic Questions

  • Is the cost issue across the entire business or a specific division/product?
  • How do our costs compare to industry benchmarks at each value chain step?
  • What are the regulatory or quality constraints on cost reduction?
  • What is the timeline for cost reduction — quick wins vs. structural changes?
📣 7. Go-To-Market (GTM) Strategy▼

When to use: Client launching a new product, entering a new channel, or re-launching an existing product.


STP — Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning

  • Segmentation: Divide market by demographic, psychographic, behavioural, geographic characteristics
  • Targeting: Select segments based on size, growth rate, competition, and fit with capabilities
  • Positioning: Define the unique value proposition — what problem do we solve uniquely for this segment?

4Ps of Marketing

  • Product: Features, quality, packaging, SKUs, branding
  • Price: Pricing strategy, introductory offers, bundle pricing
  • Place (Distribution): Online/offline mix, retail partnerships, direct sales, geographic rollout sequence
  • Promotion: Advertising (digital, print, TV), influencer marketing, PR, sampling, loyalty programmes

Launch Phases

Pre-launch (awareness building, channel seeding, beta testing) → Launch (activation, free trials, promotions, PR push) → Post-launch (feedback loop, product iteration, scale-up)

🔢 8. Guesstimate Approach▼

When to use: Estimating market sizes, volumes, revenues, or any quantitative unknown with limited data.


Six-Step Method

  1. Clarify scope: Geography, time period, product category, what is included and excluded
  2. Choose approach: Top-down (total population → filter down) or Bottom-up (unit-level → aggregate up)
  3. State assumptions aloud: "I'll assume India population = 1.4B, urban = 35%..." — this is expected and builds confidence
  4. Segment if needed: Split by income group, age group, geography to apply different penetration rates
  5. Calculate step-by-step: Walk through each step clearly, round sensibly for mental math
  6. Sanity check: Does the answer feel reasonable? Cross-check with a different approach.

🧠 9. Core Interview Skills — Hypothesis, MECE & Synthesis▼

Hypothesis-Driven Approach

Always form a hypothesis and test it, rather than asking questions randomly. Example:

"Revenue has declined. This could be due to a price decline or a volume decline. [Ask] Do we know how price and volume have changed?"

"Volume is down. This could be market size reduction or our market share declining. [Ask] Has the overall market size changed?"

"Market size is stable, so our share has dropped. [Ask] Is this across all geographies and channels, or concentrated in one area?"

MECE Segmentation

Every segmentation must be Mutually Exclusive (no overlap) and Collectively Exhaustive (covers everything). Never use trial-and-error. Always use logical structured splits: by product, by geography, by channel, by value chain stage.

Final Synthesis Template

"Based on my analysis, I recommend [X]. First, [reason 1]. Second, [reason 2]. Third, [reason 3]. The main risk to this recommendation is [Y], which I would mitigate by [Z]. If I had more time, I would also explore [additional area]."
